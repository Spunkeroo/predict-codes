Why Mechanical Keyboards for Coding

Developers type 5,000-10,000+ keystrokes per day. A good keyboard reduces finger fatigue, prevents RSI, and can genuinely increase typing speed. Mechanical switches provide tactile feedback that helps you know each keystroke registered without bottoming out, reducing force needed by 30-40% compared to cheap membrane keyboards.

Switch Types for Programming

  • Tactile (Brown, Zealio): Best for most developers. A gentle bump at actuation point provides feedback without being noisy. You feel each keypress without disturbing coworkers
  • Linear (Red, Black): Smooth press with no bump. Preferred by gamers and fast typists. Some developers find them too easy to accidentally press
  • Clicky (Blue, Box Jade): Audible click + tactile bump. Satisfying but loud. Work-from-home only unless you want office enemies
  • Topre (HHKB): Electrocapacitive — feels like typing on clouds. Expensive but beloved by programmers who try it. The HHKB is cult-favorite among senior developers

Ergonomic Considerations

Split keyboards like the ZSA Moonlander position your hands shoulder-width apart, reducing shoulder tension and ulnar deviation. They take 1-2 weeks to adjust to but most users report significant reduction in wrist and shoulder pain.

Tenting: Angling split keyboard halves inward reduces forearm pronation (twisting). Even 10-15 degrees of tenting makes a noticeable difference in comfort.
